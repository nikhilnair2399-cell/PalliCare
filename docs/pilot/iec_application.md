## 4. Background and Rationale

### 4.1 Background

Palliative care patients in India face significant challenges in symptom management between clinic visits. Pain assessment typically occurs only during outpatient consultations (every 2-4 weeks), leaving gaps in monitoring that can lead to uncontrolled symptoms, delayed interventions, and poor quality of life. The WHO estimates that over 5.5 million patients in India require palliative care annually, yet access to comprehensive palliative services remains limited.

Mobile health (mHealth) interventions have demonstrated promise in improving symptom monitoring, medication adherence, and patient-clinician communication in oncology and palliative care settings globally. However, few mHealth solutions have been designed for the Indian context, with support for Hindi language, offline functionality, and India-specific regulatory compliance (DPDPA 2023, ABDM framework).

### 4.2 Rationale

PalliCare is a bilingual (Hindi/English) mobile application designed specifically for palliative care patients, caregivers, and clinicians. It enables:

- Real-time symptom logging using validated scales (NRS, ESAS-r)
- Medication adherence tracking with Morphine Equivalent Daily Dose (MEDD) calculation
- Clinical alerts for sustained high pain, medication non-adherence, and caregiver distress
- Psychoeducation and breathing exercises for non-pharmacological symptom management
- A clinician dashboard for remote monitoring and multidisciplinary team collaboration

This pilot study will evaluate the feasibility, usability, and preliminary clinical utility of PalliCare before a larger randomized controlled trial.

### 4.3 Literature Review Summary

| Reference | Finding |
|-----------|---------|
| Basch et al., JAMA 2017 | Electronic PRO monitoring improved survival in cancer patients |
| Maguire et al., Ann Oncol 2021 | Mobile symptom monitoring reduced ER visits in advanced cancer |
| Patel et al., Indian J Palliat Care 2020 | mHealth feasibility in Indian palliative care demonstrated |
| ICMR National Guidelines, 2024 | Recommended integration of digital health tools in palliative care |

## 5. Study Objectives

### 5.1 Primary Objectives

1. **Usability**: Evaluate the usability of the PalliCare mobile application among palliative care patients and caregivers using the System Usability Scale (SUS), targeting a score of 68 or above (above-average usability).

2. **Acceptability**: Assess patient and caregiver acceptance of daily digital symptom logging and medication tracking in the palliative care context.

3. **Clinical Utility**: Determine whether PalliCare-generated data (symptom trends, medication adherence reports, clinical alerts) is perceived as clinically useful by the palliative care team.

### 5.2 Secondary Objectives

1. Measure patient engagement: logging frequency, feature utilization, and retention over 4 weeks
2. Assess the technical reliability of the application (crash rate, data sync success, offline performance)
3. Evaluate the impact of symptom logging on patient-reported pain awareness and management
4. Measure medication adherence rates as captured by the application
5. Document caregiver experience with proxy symptom logging and self-care features
6. Identify barriers and facilitators to mHealth adoption in the Indian palliative care setting

## 7. Sample Size and Selection

### 7.1 Sample Size Justification

This is a pilot feasibility study. Following recommendations by Julious (2005) and Lancaster et al. (2004) for pilot studies, a sample of 30 patients is sufficient to estimate feasibility parameters with reasonable precision, evaluate usability, and identify major implementation barriers.

| Group | Target N | Rationale |
|-------|----------|-----------|
| Patients | 30 | Sufficient for SUS estimation (SD ~15, 95% CI width ~11) |
| Caregivers | 15 | Primary caregivers of enrolled patients (proxy logging evaluation) |
| Clinicians | 5-8 | Palliative care team members (dashboard evaluation) |

### 7.2 Inclusion Criteria (Patients)

1. Age 18 years or older
2. Diagnosed with advanced cancer or chronic non-cancer condition requiring palliative care
3. Under active follow-up at AIIMS Bhopal palliative care or pain management clinic
4. Expected prognosis of 3 months or more (as assessed by treating clinician)
5. Access to an Android smartphone (personal or shared with caregiver)
6. Able to read Hindi or English, or has a caregiver willing to assist with app use
7. Willing to log symptoms at least 3 times per week for 4 weeks
8. Provides written informed consent

### 7.3 Inclusion Criteria (Caregivers)

1. Age 18 years or older
2. Identified as the primary caregiver of an enrolled patient
3. Access to an Android smartphone
4. Willing to assist with symptom logging and/or use caregiver features
5. Provides written informed consent

### 7.4 Exclusion Criteria

1. Severe cognitive impairment or delirium preventing meaningful app interaction
2. Unstable clinical condition requiring immediate intensive care or critical care admission
3. No access to an Android smartphone (patient or caregiver)
4. Unable or unwilling to provide informed consent
5. Currently enrolled in another mHealth-based clinical study
6. Anticipated poor compliance due to social or logistical factors (at investigator's discretion)

### 7.5 Recruitment Strategy

1. Screen palliative care outpatient clinic attendees during routine visits
2. Clinician referral from pain management clinic consultations
3. Identify eligible inpatients receiving palliative care consults (for outpatient follow-up enrollment)
4. Approach primary caregivers of enrolled patients for caregiver arm
5. No advertisements or incentives for recruitment (token travel reimbursement only)

## 8. Intervention Description

### 8.1 The PalliCare Application

PalliCare is a mobile health application with the following components:

**Patient Mobile App (Android)**:
- Symptom logging: Numeric Rating Scale (NRS) 0-10 for pain, 28-zone body map, pain quality descriptors, Edmonton Symptom Assessment System - Revised (ESAS-r)
- Medication tracker: Dose logging, schedule reminders, PRN (as-needed) tracking, side effect reporting
- Mood and sleep tracking: 5-point emoji mood scale, sleep quality assessment
- Breathing exercises: Guided breathing programs (4-7-8, box breathing, pranayama)
- Psychoeducation: Learn modules on pain management, symptom relief, and palliative care concepts
- Caregiver mode: Proxy symptom logging, wellness self-check, burnout assessment
- Bilingual: Full Hindi and English support
- Offline-first: All features work without internet; data syncs when connectivity is available

**Clinician Web Dashboard**:
- Patient list with traffic-light triage (red/yellow/green based on NRS severity)
- Individual patient views: pain trends, medication review, MEDD tracking, ESAS summaries
- Clinical alerts: Automated alerts for sustained high pain, medication non-adherence, MEDD thresholds
- SBAR handover generation for multidisciplinary team rounds
- Analytics and report generation

### 8.2 Participant Workflow

1. **Enrollment visit**: Informed consent, app installation, 30-minute training session
2. **Daily use (4 weeks)**: Patients log symptoms (minimum 3 times/week), track medication intake
3. **Weekly check**: Research coordinator contacts participant by phone if logging < 3 times/week
4. **Week 4**: Exit survey (SUS + satisfaction questionnaire), optional semi-structured interview

## 10. Risk Assessment

### 10.1 Risk Classification

**This study is classified as MINIMAL RISK.**

The intervention involves use of a mobile application for symptom self-reporting and medication tracking. The app does not replace medical care, prescribe medications, or make clinical decisions. All participants continue to receive standard palliative care from their treating team.

### 10.2 Potential Risks and Mitigations

| Risk | Likelihood | Severity | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|----------|------------|
| Emotional distress from symptom tracking | Low | Mild | Breathing exercises and educational content in-app; participants can skip any log entry; clinical support available |
| Time burden | Low | Mild | Quick Log feature requires < 30 seconds; minimum expectation is 3 logs/week |
| Data breach / privacy concern | Very low | Moderate | End-to-end encryption (AES-256 at rest, TLS 1.3 in transit); anonymized analytics; DPDPA 2023 compliance |
| Delayed medical care due to app reliance | Very low | Serious | Prominent disclaimers that app does not replace medical care; emergency number (112) in app; clinical alerts to care team |
| Device loss or theft | Low | Mild | App requires authentication (OTP); data encrypted on device; remote wipe capability |
| Technical failure / data loss | Very low | Mild | Offline-first architecture; local data backup on device (Hive database); server backup |

### 10.3 Safety Monitoring

- Weekly review of adverse events by PI
- Any app-related safety concern reported to PI within 24 hours
- Serious adverse events (SAE) reported to IEC per AIIMS Bhopal protocol
- Data Safety Monitoring not required (minimal risk pilot, no randomization)

## 11. Data Privacy and Protection

### 11.1 Regulatory Framework

This study complies with:
- **Digital Personal Data Protection Act (DPDPA), 2023** (Government of India)
- **Indian Council of Medical Research (ICMR) National Ethical Guidelines for Biomedical and Health Research Involving Human Participants, 2017**
- **AIIMS Bhopal Institutional Ethics Committee Standard Operating Procedures**
- **Information Technology Act, 2000 (IT Act)** and IT (Reasonable Security Practices) Rules, 2011

### 11.2 Data Security Measures

| Measure | Implementation |
|---------|----------------|
| Encryption at rest | AES-256 encryption for all stored data (device and server) |
| Encryption in transit | TLS 1.3 for all network communication |
| Authentication | OTP-based phone authentication; session tokens with expiry |
| Access control | Role-based access: patients see own data only; clinicians see assigned patients; researchers see anonymized datasets |
| Audit logging | All data access logged with timestamp, user ID, and action |
| Anonymization | Analytics data stripped of all personally identifiable information (PII) before aggregation |
| Data minimization | Only clinically necessary data fields collected; no location tracking, no contacts access |
| Physical security | Server hosted on encrypted cloud infrastructure with ISO 27001 compliance |

### 11.3 Data Retention

- Clinical data: Retained for 6 months post-study completion for analysis, then reviewed for archival or deletion
- Anonymized analytics: Retained for up to 2 years for research publication purposes
- Informed consent forms: Retained per AIIMS Bhopal IEC requirements (minimum 5 years)
- Interview recordings: Transcribed, de-identified, then recordings deleted within 3 months of study completion

### 11.4 Participant Data Rights (under DPDPA 2023)

Participants have the right to:
1. Access their personal data collected during the study
2. Request correction of inaccurate data
3. Request deletion of their data (right to erasure)
4. Export their data in a machine-readable format
5. Withdraw consent for data processing at any time

## 12. Informed Consent Process

### 12.1 Consent Procedure

1. Potential participants are identified during routine palliative care consultations
2. The research coordinator explains the study in the participant's preferred language (Hindi or English)
3. The participant is given the informed consent form to read (or it is read aloud if needed)
4. The participant has the opportunity to ask questions and discuss with family members
5. A minimum cooling-off period of 24 hours is provided before signing (unless the participant wishes to consent immediately)
6. Written informed consent is obtained before any study-related procedures
7. A copy of the signed consent form is provided to the participant

### 12.2 Consent Documents

- Informed consent form in English (Annexure A)
- Informed consent form in Hindi (Annexure B)
- Participant information sheet (bilingual)

### 12.3 Special Considerations

- For participants with limited literacy: Consent form is read aloud in full; an independent witness signs the form
- For caregiver consent: Separate consent form for caregivers participating in the caregiver arm
- Participants may withdraw consent at any time without affecting their medical care

## 13. Expected Outcomes

### 13.1 Primary Outcomes

1. **Usability**: SUS score of 68 or above (above-average usability threshold), indicating the app is suitable for broader deployment
2. **Acceptability**: At least 70% of participants rate the app as "useful" or "very useful" for symptom management
3. **Clinical Utility**: At least 80% of clinicians rate the dashboard data as "clinically useful" for patient management

### 13.2 Secondary Outcomes

1. Engagement: Average of 3 or more symptom logs per patient per week
2. Retention: At least 70% of enrolled patients complete the 4-week pilot
3. Technical stability: Application crash rate below 2%
4. Medication adherence: Self-reported medication adherence rate as captured by the app
5. Pain trends: Descriptive analysis of NRS score trajectories over 4 weeks
6. Qualitative themes: Barriers, facilitators, and suggestions for improvement

### 13.3 Dissemination Plan

- Internal report to Department of Anaesthesiology & Palliative Medicine, AIIMS Bhopal
- Presentation at departmental research meeting
- Submission to Indian Journal of Palliative Care or BMJ Supportive & Palliative Care
- Conference presentation at IAPCON / ISSP annual conference
- Results will inform the design of a subsequent multi-center randomized controlled trial

## 14. Ethical Considerations

### 14.1 Beneficence

The study aims to improve symptom monitoring and communication for palliative care patients. Participants may directly benefit from better pain tracking and medication adherence support.

### 14.2 Non-maleficence

The study is minimal risk. The app supplements (does not replace) standard medical care. Safety nets include emergency helpline access, clinical alerts, and weekly safety reviews.

### 14.3 Autonomy

Participation is voluntary. Informed consent is obtained in the participant's preferred language. Participants may withdraw at any time without any impact on their medical care.

### 14.4 Justice

Recruitment is from the existing palliative care patient population at AIIMS Bhopal. No population is unfairly burdened. The bilingual design ensures Hindi-speaking patients are not excluded.

### 14.5 Conflict of Interest

The investigators declare no financial conflict of interest. The PalliCare application is developed as an academic project with no commercial intent during the pilot phase.
